# Policy context: Spain 2026 extraordinary regularisation process

Last reviewed: 2026-04-15  
Reviewed by: Jo

## Purpose

This document records the current external policy position shaping the regularisation intake screener.

It is not legal advice and it is not a substitute for the BOE text, official government guidance, or case-specific advice. It exists so policy-sensitive assumptions can be reviewed and updated without rewriting the design specification.

## Current context

As of 15 April 2026, Spain's extraordinary regularisation process is no longer just a proposed policy direction. It is now part of the active legal framework through [Real Decreto 316/2026, de 14 de abril](https://www.boe.es/eli/es/rd/2026/04/14/316), published in the BOE on 15 April 2026 and entering into force on 16 April 2026.

The current framework creates two temporary routes relevant to this product:

1. a route for people who **presented or formalised an application for international protection in Spain before 1 January 2026**
2. a route for people who **were in Spain before 1 January 2026** and may qualify for **arraigo extraordinario**

Both routes sit inside the immigration regulation as temporary residence authorisations for exceptional circumstances under the amended [Reglamento de Extranjería approved by Real Decreto 1155/2024](https://www.boe.es/buscar/act.php?id=BOE-A-2024-24099).

For both routes, the current legal position includes core requirements around:

- presence in Spain before **1 January 2026**
- **five months of uninterrupted stay** before the application is filed
- absence of disqualifying **criminal-record issues**
- not representing a threat to **public order, public security, or public health**

The extraordinary process is time-limited. Under [Real Decreto 316/2026](https://www.boe.es/eli/es/rd/2026/04/14/316), applications for both routes may be submitted until **30 June 2026**.

The residence authorisation granted under these routes has a validity of **one year** and allows work **throughout Spain**, both **employed** and **self-employed**, subject to the terms of the decree.

## Current operational context

The policy context now has a stronger operational support layer than before.

Spain has created a formal [Registro Electrónico de Colaboradores de Extranjería](https://www.boe.es/buscar/doc.php?id=BOE-A-2026-5128), regulated by [Orden ISM/164/2026, de 2 de marzo](https://www.boe.es/eli/es/o/2026/03/02/ism164). This allows certain unions and non-profit organisations working in migration or international protection to assist people with regularisation-related procedures.

Official government communication also makes clear that collaborator entities are part of the intended delivery model for the regularisation process, not a marginal pathway. See [La Moncloa: Migraciones abre el registro de colaboradores de extranjería para impulsar la regularización extraordinaria](https://www.lamoncloa.gob.es/serviciosdeprensa/notasprensa/inclusion/Paginas/2026/090326-registro-colaboradores-extranjeria.aspx).

## What has changed since the previous version

The previous version of this document described the regularisation as an emerging policy context shaped by government statements and likely criteria.

That is now out of date.

The product should now treat the regularisation as an **active legal and administrative process** with:

- a published legal instrument
- an entry-into-force date
- live submission routes
- an application deadline
- explicit route distinctions inside the regulation

The product should no longer imply that the process is merely anticipated, provisional, or only politically announced.

## Product assumptions

The product currently assumes that a useful first-touch screener should help establish:

- whether someone appears to fall within one of the two active temporary routes
- whether the person reports being in Spain before **1 January 2026**
- whether the person reports **five months of continuous stay** before applying
- whether the person appears closer to the **international protection applicant** route or the **arraigo extraordinario** route
- whether the person reports identity documents that may support an application
- whether the person reports evidence that may help show date of presence and recent continuity
- whether criminal-record issues, asylum complexity, identity issues, or vulnerability make generic routing unsafe
- what kind of support or referral is needed next

These remain **triage assumptions only**. The service must still not present itself as:

- deciding legal entitlement
- predicting approval with certainty
- replacing official instructions
- replacing specialist legal or case advice

## Product constraints created by the current policy context

The current policy position implies that:

- the service must distinguish more clearly between the **international protection** route and the **arraigo extraordinario** route
- the service should treat **presence before 1 January 2026** and **five months of uninterrupted stay before application** as central triage inputs
- the service should treat **criminal-record risk** and broader **public-order risk** as high-priority specialist signals
- the service should treat **document readiness** as operationally important because the process is evidence-based rather than self-declaration-only
- the service should recognise that **vulnerability**, **family-unit ties**, and **work or intention to work** now matter directly within the extraordinary route
- the service should assume that assisted completion and partner handoff are part of the real operating model, especially where collaborator entities are involved

## Additional route-level implications

The current BOE text indicates that:

- people in the international protection route must have presented or formalised that application **before 1 January 2026**
- people in the extraordinary route must have been **in Spain before 1 January 2026**
- both routes require **five months of uninterrupted stay** before application
- both routes require lack of disqualifying criminal-record issues and no threat to public order, public security, or public health
- both routes can be requested until **30 June 2026**
- both routes include provisional permission to reside and work from the start of processing, subject to the terms of the decree
- the extraordinary route includes pathways based on:
  - having worked or intending to work
  - living in Spain with a qualifying family unit
  - being in a situation of vulnerability accredited by competent bodies or, in some cases, by third-sector entities registered in the collaborators register

The current BOE text also indicates that the extraordinary route is **not** for people who are or have been holders of residence authorisation under the temporary protection scheme linked to [Council Implementing Decision (EU) 2022/382](https://eur-lex.europa.eu/eli/dec_impl/2022/382/oj).

## Implications for user-facing triage

The screener should now align with the active process rather than a generic policy forecast.

That means the journey should:

- ask whether the person is **in Spain now**
- ask whether the person was **already in Spain before 1 January 2026**
- ask about **continuous stay during the five months before application**
- ask whether the person had an **international protection application in Spain before 1 January 2026**
- ask about **identity documents** in a way that maps to the current legal requirements
- ask about **evidence categories** that may help show both earlier presence and recent continuity
- ask about **vulnerability**, **family responsibilities**, and **work-related situation** where relevant to routing
- keep using specialist routing for criminal-record issues, asylum complexity, identity mismatch, safeguarding concerns, and high uncertainty
- avoid overpromising where the user reports weak evidence, contradictory history, or unclear status

## Operational assumptions for support and referral

The support ecosystem matters materially to the current process.

The regularisation now has an explicit relationship with registered collaborator organisations. The product should therefore assume that:

- many users will complete the screener with help from an NGO, union, volunteer, or support worker
- assisted completion is not an edge case
- referral pathways should be designed as part of the core journey
- support-oriented summaries may be operationally useful for handoff
- vulnerability-led cases may require faster and more specialist support than standard self-serve users

## Change rule

When policy or official guidance changes:

1. update this document first
2. review affected triage rules
3. review affected user-facing copy and result text
4. review whether route questions still reflect the active legal requirements
5. record the review date and reviewer
