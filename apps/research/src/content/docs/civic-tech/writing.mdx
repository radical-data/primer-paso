---
title: Writing for Civic Services
description: Writing clearly for people navigating stressful administrative systems
---

Good civic-service writing reduces fear, not just confusion.

## Write for the moment the user is in

People using Primer Paso may be worried about legal status, family, work, documents, deadlines, or institutions. Writing should assume stress without becoming patronising.

## Say what the system knows

Avoid vague reassurance. Be explicit about what the service can and cannot determine.

## Separate eligibility from certainty

A guide can help someone identify a likely route. It should not imply that approval is guaranteed.

## Use plain language for legal concepts

Legal precision matters, but legalistic wording often transfers the burden of interpretation to the user.

## Avoid false finality

Prefer:

> Based on these answers, this route may be relevant.

Avoid:

> You are eligible.

## Explain why questions are asked

Users are more likely to trust sensitive questions when the service explains why the answer matters.

## Write useful errors

Errors should help users recover, not make them feel at fault.

## Support bilingual checking

Many users will switch languages to compare wording, especially when the consequence feels serious.

## Good patterns

- “You may need…”
- “This does not guarantee…”
- “You can use this answer to…”
- “Ask a qualified organisation if…”
- “You do not need this document for this route.”

## Bad patterns

- “Invalid”
- “You failed to…”
- “Simply upload…”
- “Just book an appointment…”
- “Obviously…”
